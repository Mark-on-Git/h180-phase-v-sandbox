HANGOVR 180 is a capsule dietary supplement for responsible adults who already choose to drink and want to support next-day wellness. It is regulated under DSHEA / 21 CFR 101.93. It makes no disease claim of any kind.

This category has long relied on overpromising, blurry claims, and wink-wink marketing. Consumers deserve a higher standard. We built this brand to be that standard -- not to apologize for the category, but to correct it. What follows is the documentation of how.

We choose not to claim that HANGOVR 180 prevents intoxication, reduces impairment, or makes alcohol consumption safe. We choose not to position it as permission to drink more. We choose not to make disease claims, detox claims, or alcohol-safety claims. We choose to operate only within the structure/function claim pathway Congress created under DSHEA -- support for normal body structure and function in the context of moderate alcohol consumption. That is the standard we chose. It is not the minimum the category requires. It is the one we require of ourselves.

The name is direct because the occasion is direct. HANGOVR 180 refers to the familiar reality of wanting tomorrow to feel like yourself after a social occasion. The dropped letter is deliberate -- an artistic choice, not an error. The 180 is directional. A brand name can refer to a real consumer occasion without making an impermissible health claim. Our claims are governed by what we actually say, what the product is, and how it is positioned -- not by what a name might be assumed to imply. By the unanimous determination of the United States Supreme Court in Matal v. Tam, 582 U.S. 218 (2017), reaffirmed in Iancu v. Brunetti, 588 U.S. 388 (2019), trademarks constitute protected private speech and the perceived implication of a mark cannot be the basis for suppressing or penalizing it.

The submission program and claims strategy were designed by Dr. Corey Hilmas, formerly Team Leader of the FDA's Dietary Supplement Regulation Implementation Team, engaged as an independent consultant through KGK Science. The claims themselves were developed independently by Next Level Brands. 35 structure/function claims submitted under DSHEA / 21 CFR 101.93. One objected to -- permanently excluded from all company communications in any form. 34 remain without objection. Those 34 cover: support for alcohol and aldehyde metabolism; cellular protection against alcohol-related free radical damage; glutathione and mitochondrial support; liver health; mood balance; occasional anxiety support specific to alcohol-induced GABA activation; and balanced alcohol consumption.

The following reflects conclusions of peer-reviewed published research. Alcohol binds to GABA-A receptors during intoxication, artificially suppressing neural excitability. On clearance, GABA sensitivity undershoots baseline and glutamate surges -- a rebound state documented as a driver of next-day anxiety. DHM has been shown to modulate GABA-A receptor activity. Ethanol metabolism produces acetaldehyde as a primary intermediate; when the ADH/ALDH enzymatic pathway is overwhelmed, acetaldehyde accumulates and is more toxic than ethanol itself. DHM has been shown to support ADH and ALDH enzyme activity. Ethanol actively inhibits glutathione synthesis through suppression of the cysteine transport mechanism required for GSH production -- this is not depletion through oxidative demand, the synthesis pathway itself is inhibited while ethanol is present. DHM has been shown to support mitochondrial function and mitochondrial biogenesis.

The following reflects operating conclusions of Next Level Brands, reasoned from published science and pre-commercial testing -- not demonstrated in controlled clinical trial. The GABA rebound state and anxiety as a documented driver of alcohol craving are both established in published research. That the neurochemical disruption from drinking today increases the pull toward drinking tomorrow is Next Level Brands' conclusion drawn from those two adjacent truths -- acted on, not yet proven in controlled trial. Published studies demonstrate that humic and fulvic acids increase the bioavailability of flavonols; DHM is a flavonoid; pre-commercial testing showed a coverage benefit congruent with enhanced bioavailability. The conclusion that fulvic acid increases DHM bioavailability in this formula is an operating conclusion. Fulvic acid is included at 150mg, 90% purity, on that basis.

The claim architecture is reviewed, disclosed, bounded, and available for inspection at every level of this site. The name is art. The product is science. The company is serious. Their evidence is public.
